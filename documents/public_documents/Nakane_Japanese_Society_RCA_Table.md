| 1 | Tate (vertical) | 縦 | Core concept | The vertical dimension of human relations — hierarchical, transgenerational, organized by seniority and role succession. Nakane's primary analytical axis. Tate relations persist across time and generations; they are not dissolved when current members leave. | **Maps to V-axis** — transgenerational continuity as structural axis | Ch.1 (RCA: direct) |
| 2 | Yoko (horizontal) | 横 | Core concept | The horizontal dimension — peer relations among equals, same-generation, same-rank, same-attribute. In Nakane's analysis, yoko relations are structurally subordinate to tate in Japanese society; horizontal class solidarity is weak. | **Maps to H-axis** — present-moment peer relations | Ch.1 (RCA: direct) |
| 3 | Tate shakai (vertical society) | 縦社会 | Central thesis | Nakane's thesis: Japanese society is organized primarily along the tate axis, not the yoko axis. Vertical solidarity within a group is stronger than horizontal solidarity across groups of same status. | **V ⊥ H asymmetry** — confirms V and H are analytically independent; Japan structurally prioritizes V | Ch.1–2 (RCA: direct) |
| 4 | Ba (frame) | 場 | Structural concept | The situational frame — the organization, institution, or group — that defines membership in Japanese society. Nakane contrasts "frame" (ba) with "attribute" (Western model): Japanese primary identity is frame-based (which organization), not attribute-based (which profession/class). | Supports V-axis as structural (not merely cultural) — ie, company, school are transgenerational frames | Ch.1 (RCA: direct) |
| 5 | Ie (household/house) | 家 | Social structure | The household as a corporate, transgenerational unit. The ie is not the living members but the *house* persisting through time — current members are stewards, not owners. The ie exists before and after any individual member. | **V-axis structural evidence** — ie demonstrates V-axis is ontological (not just cultural practice); the unit of identity transcends present-moment membership | Ch.1–2 (RCA: synthesized) |
| 6 | Oyabun-kobun | 親分–子分 | Relational pattern | Patron-client relationship modeled on parent-child (oya = parent, ko = child) — a tate relation not based on biological kinship but on role succession and care obligation. Transmits across time: kobun becomes oyabun to the next generation. | V-axis transmission mechanism — role succession as V-axis propagation without biological requirement | Ch.2 (RCA: direct) |
| 7 | Seniority (nenkō) | 年功序列 | Organizational principle | Rank determined by time-in-role (years of experience within the frame), not by achievement or attribute. Time accumulates along tate axis — the longer in the system, the higher the rank. | V-axis operationalized as measurable variable — duration in V-axis position determines status | Ch.2–3 (RCA: synthesized) |
| 8 | Uchi / Soto | 内 / 外 | Boundary concept | Inside (uchi) vs. outside (soto) — the boundary of the frame. Those in uchi share tate obligations; those in soto do not. Boundary is maintained by the frame, not by attribute or class. | Maps to Tiên Đề IV (Dynamic Boundary) — selective permeability based on frame membership; corroborates IV cross-culturally | Ch.1 (RCA: secondary-confirmed) |
| 9 | Tate independence from yoko | — | Analytic claim | Nakane's key structural observation: tate and yoko are **analytically independent** organizing principles — raising one does not reduce the other. Strong tate ≠ weak yoko as a mathematical necessity; they operate on different dimensions. | **V ⊥ H mathematical independence** — Nakane's claim implies orthogonality: two dimensions, not two ends of one spectrum | Ch.1–2 (RCA: synthesized) |
| 10 | Group competition along tate | — | Dynamics | Within a Japanese group (ba), competition occurs vertically — members compete for higher tate positions, not for horizontal class mobility. Cross-group horizontal solidarity is suppressed by vertical in-group loyalty. | V-axis as primary identity axis — H-axis (horizontal class) is secondary; confirms V and H are not merely named differently but have different functional roles | Ch.3 (RCA: direct) |
| 11 | Sempai-kōhai | 先輩–後輩 | Relational pattern | Senior-junior relation within the same frame — based on entry time, not authority. Sempai (entered earlier) holds informal guidance role over kōhai (entered later). Relation is permanent and persists even when both leave the frame. | V-axis persistence — tate relations follow persons across contexts; V-axis is not frame-local but person-carried | Ch.2 (RCA: secondary-confirmed) |
| 12 | Frame over attribute | — | Central thesis | In Western models, identity = attribute (I am a doctor → horizontal solidarity with doctors). In Nakane's Japan, identity = frame (I am from Tōdai → vertical solidarity with Tōdai seniors). Frame identity suppresses attribute (class) identity. | Confirms V-axis primacy — the frame (ba) = vertical institution; attribute = horizontal category. Japanese structure confirms V ⊥ H are genuinely different axes, not correlated | Ch.1 (RCA: direct) |
