## Scoring Rubric — Short Film Suitability (5 tiêu chí, mỗi 1 điểm)

| # | Tiêu chí | Câu hỏi | Điểm cao = |
|---|----------|---------|------------|
| **C1** | **Visual narrativity** | Can the entire emotional arc be told through images alone? | Camera, composition, light, movement carry 100% of narrative |
| **C2** | **Dialogue independence** | Can it work with 0–5 lines of dialogue total? | Silent film possible; images do all the work |
| **C3** | **Compressibility** | Can the core dramatic arc fit in 5 minutes? | 1 situation, 1–2 locations, 1–2 characters, single emotional beat |
| **C4** | **Immediate impact** | Does the central image hit in the first 30 seconds without setup? | Viewer feels it before understanding it |
| **C5** | **Single central image** | Is there ONE visual that anchors the entire short? | One image = the whole film; the short IS that image |

**Ngưỡng:** ≥ 4/5 → TOP 10 candidate. 5/5 → Tier 1 (perfect short film).

---

## 3-Round RCA — Why These Criteria?

**Round 1 — Symptom:** Many feature-film ideas collapse when compressed to 5 minutes — they rely on dialogue exposition, multi-character arcs, or complex setups.

**Round 2 — Mechanism:** A 5-minute short film is not a "mini feature." It's a different medium. The audience doesn't have time to learn backstory, understand relationships, or follow dialogue. The only thing that works is: **ONE image → ONE emotional transformation**. The image does the work; dialogue is decoration.

**Round 3 — Root:** Short films are a **pure visual medium**. The viewer must *feel* the Axiom 3 mechanism (V⊥H connection/disconnection) through their eyes, not through explanation. A script that requires dialogue to explain the V⊥H breakage fails the short film test.

## #1 — NỀN NHÀ / The Foundation
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · Hà Nội`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Man enters abandoned house. Altar visible. Incense still burning.
- 1:00–2:30 — He works: measuring, marking walls for demolition. Each time he passes the altar, he slows.
- 2:30–3:30 — He stands before the altar. Doesn't know how to light incense. Just stands. 45 seconds.
- 3:30–4:30 — He reaches out. Wraps the incense bowl in his jacket. Carries it out.
- 4:30–5:00 — Exterior. Excavator waiting. He walks past it. Bowl cradled in his arms.

**Central image:** A construction worker's rough hand — the hand that holds a demolition hammer — trembling as it touches a porcelain incense bowl.

**Why it works silently:** The altar with burning incense IS the opening hook. No explanation needed. Every Vietnamese viewer understands immediately: someone was just here. Someone is still here. The conflict is in the man's hands — demolition tools vs. sacred object. No words.

## #2 — BÀI HÁT RU / The Lullaby
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5`
`2 locations · 2 actors · 0 dialogue · Cần Thơ`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Old hands signing a lullaby to a child. Child's hands mirroring. (No sound — the world is silent.)
- 1:00–2:00 — Funeral. The old hands are still. The child watches. Looks at her own hands.
- 2:00–3:30 — Child tries to sign the lullaby alone. Her hands falter. No one watches.
- 3:30–4:30 — Child takes her mother's hands. Places them on her own chest. Begins to sign. Mother's hands begin to mirror.
- 4:30–5:00 — Two pairs of hands — one large, one small — moving in the same rhythm. The lullaby, passed upward for the first time in 4 generations.

**Central image:** Two pairs of hands moving in identical rhythm — a lullaby "sung" from child to mother, reversing 4 generations of transmission.

**Why it works silently:** The entire film takes place in a deaf child's world — which is already silent. Sign language IS visual language. The "music" is movement. The loss is stillness. The recovery is two hands finding the same rhythm.

## #3 — TRÁI MÙA / Out of Season
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5`
`2 locations · 1 actor · 0–3 lines · Bắc Ninh`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:30 — Temple. One long take. Woman sings quan họ. Natural light. Birds. Wind. A few villagers listen. This is "right."
- 1:30–2:30 — Cut to: Festival stage. Beer logos. Plastic chairs. Tourists talking. Woman approaches microphone. She opens her mouth —
- 2:30–3:00 — Silence. 15 seconds. She cannot sing.
- 3:00–4:00 — She steps off stage. Camera stays on stage, watching confused audience. We don't see where she goes.
- 4:00–5:00 — Temple. She sits alone. Not singing. Just sitting. Right space. Right season. This time she is the listener.

**Central image:** A woman opens her mouth on a festival stage and produces only silence — then walks away, and the camera doesn't follow.

**Why it works silently:** The contrast between "right singing" (long take, natural sound, temple) and "wrong singing" (silence, noise, stage) is purely cinematic. The audience HEARS the difference before they understand it. The silence on stage is the loudest moment in the film.

## #4 — CHUNG TƯỜNG / Shared Wall
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 2 actors · 0 dialogue · Hội An`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Establishing: the wall. The crack. Two altars facing each other through it. Two families visible on each side.
- 1:00–2:00 — Both families light incense. Smoke rises on both sides.
- 2:00–3:00 — Smoke from side A seeps through the crack. Smoke from side B seeps through. They meet above — merging into one stream.
- 3:00–4:00 — A boy on side A. A girl on side B. They notice each other through the crack. Light through the gap. They don't speak. They look.
- 4:00–5:00 — The two streams of smoke have merged completely above the wall. The adults don't see it. The children do.

**Central image:** Two streams of incense smoke from two enemy family altars, rising and merging into one above a cracked wall — seen only by two teenagers in love.

**Why it works silently:** The wall does all the storytelling. The crack is the script. The smoke is the dialogue. Two families that haven't spoken in 50 years = the film itself has no speaking. The wall, the altars, the incense, the children's eyes — every element is visual.

## #5 — CA ĐÊM / Night Shift
`Source: rca_axiom3_film_slate.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · Hà Nội Phố Cổ`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:30 — 4:00 AM. Woman starts her routine: lights fire, stirs broth, sets up stall. Incense on small altar. Her lips move — silent conversation with ancestors.
- 1:30–2:30 — Regular customers arrive. They eat. They leave. Routine. The rhythm of 40 years.
- 2:30–3:30 — She notices: demolition notice posted on the wall next to her altar. She reads it. Returns to cooking.
- 3:30–4:30 — 11:00 AM. She closes the stall. Cleans. Last to leave. Stands before the altar.
- 4:30–5:00 — She touches the altar. Her hand lingers. Then she turns off the light. The altar's incense dot glows red in darkness.

**Central image:** At 4:15 AM, steam rising from a 4-generation broth, a woman's lips move in silent conversation with her great-grandmother — a meeting that has happened every morning for 40 years.

**Why it works silently:** The routine IS the film. Every action is visual: stirring, pouring, wiping, lighting incense. The demolition notice is a visual prop — no one needs to say "we're being evicted." The silent conversation with ancestors through lip movements = the Axiom 3 interface shown, not told.

## #6 — SÔNG MẸ / Mother River
`Source: rca_axiom3_film_slate.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · ĐBSCL / An Giang`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Dawn mist. A farmer walks into his rice field. Water is at his ankles.
- 1:00–2:00 — He checks the dikes. Water has risen past last year's mark. Past the 1995 mark. Past the 1978 mark. He touches the marks on the pillar.
- 2:00–3:30 — He continues his ritual: feeling the soil, checking the rice. Water now at his knees. He does not hurry. This is not panic — this is farewell.
- 3:30–4:30 — Wide shot. One man, waist-deep in water, still moving through his field. Checking what cannot be saved.
- 4:30–5:00 — He stops. Looks out at the horizon — where river meets sky. His face: not defeat. Recognition.

**Central image:** A farmer wading through his flooded rice field at dawn, touching the water-level marks carved into his house pillar — each mark a generation, each mark now underwater.

**Why it works silently:** The landscape is the protagonist. Water is the antagonist. The marks on the pillar are the only "text" — and they tell the entire history. The man's body in the water = Axiom 3: he stays connected to the land even as the land disappears, because his ancestors are IN the land.

## #7 — NHỮNG NGÓN TAY / The Hands
`Source: rca_axiom3_film_slate.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 2 actors · 0 dialogue · Bắc Ninh (Đông Hồ)`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Close-up: old hands pressing a woodblock onto dó paper. The rhythm is perfect. 200 years of muscle memory.
- 1:00–2:00 — The old hands stop. Rest on the woodblock. The son watches from the doorway.
- 2:00–3:00 — Son approaches. Picks up the woodblock. His hands try — wrong pressure. The print is uneven. Tries again — wrong angle.
- 3:00–4:00 — Father stands behind him. Doesn't instruct. Just watches. Son takes a breath. Presses. This time — the pressure is right. The print comes out perfect.
- 4:00–5:00 — Father looks at the print. Looks at son's hands. No words. The hands have spoken.

**Central image:** A son's hand pressing a 200-year-old woodblock — and the moment the pressure becomes exactly right, neither too hard nor too soft, and the print emerges perfect.

**Why it works silently:** The entire drama is in HANDS. Old hands → young hands → the transfer of pressure. The woodblock is the Axiom 3 interface: pressing it correctly means connecting with every hand that has pressed it before. No dialogue needed — the print itself is the proof of connection.

## #8 — MÙI NHÀ / The Scent of Home
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5`
`2 locations · 1 actor · 0–2 lines · Việt Trì`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Woman walks through unfamiliar Vietnamese town. She doesn't speak the language. She's a stranger in her birthplace.
- 1:00–2:00 — She carries an urn (father's ashes). Her mission: scatter them. She looks lost.
- 2:00–3:30 — She approaches a paper mill. Stops. Something shifts in her posture.
- 3:30–4:30 — Wind blows from the mill. Carries the smell of wet paper pulp. She closes her eyes. Her shoulders drop — for the first time. Her breathing slows. Her body recognizes what her mind cannot.
- 4:30–5:00 — She stands. Eyes closed. The urn in her hands. She has arrived. Not at a place. At a smell.

**Central image:** A woman, eyes closed, standing at a factory gate — shoulders finally relaxed, breathing finally slow — her body has come home before her mind knows what "home" means.

**Why it works near-silently:** The drama is purely sensory: the body's response to smell. Camera shows the transformation through posture, breath, muscle tension. No flashback. No "I remember." The body tells the story.

## #9 — GIẾNG CŨ / The Well
`Source: rca_axiom3_screenplays.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · Thái Bình (Bắc Bộ)`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Night. An old well, capped with concrete. Water seeps through cracks. Drip. Drip.
- 1:00–2:00 — A woman (architect) approaches. Studies the well. Touches the wet concrete.
- 2:00–3:00 — She looks down into the water that has pooled on top. Sees her own reflection.
- 3:00–4:00 — The reflection shifts. Another woman's face appears behind hers. Not threatening. Just... there. Two faces in the water. Present and past.
- 4:00–5:00 — Dawn. The woman is still there. The water has risen slightly. Two faces are now one.

**Central image:** A woman looking into well water and seeing not just her own reflection but another woman's face behind hers — two generations superimposed in a mirror of water.

**Why it works silently:** Folk horror aesthetics. The well is the Axiom 3 interface: concrete tried to cap it, but water still seeps through. The reflection = V⊥H made visible. No jump scares. No dialogue. Just water, face, face-within-face.

## #10 — NUNG NẾP ĐẤT / Firing the Invariant
`Source: script_ideas_v2.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · Làng gốm ven sông Hồng`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — A woman's hands working clay. The wheel spins. The clay rises.
- 1:00–2:00 — She opens an old kiln. Inside: broken pots from 1982. Her father's "failed" batch. She examines the crack patterns — they're deliberate. Not failures. Signatures.
- 2:00–3:30 — She begins replicating the "failed" glaze technique. Mixing. Testing. Firing. The kiln glows orange in darkness.
- 3:30–4:30 — She opens the kiln. The new pot has the same deliberate crack pattern. Her hand traces the crack — the same movement her father's hand made.
- 4:30–5:00 — The pot sits in morning light. The crack catches the sun. Beauty in what was called failure.

**Central image:** A woman's hand tracing a deliberate crack in a ceramic pot — the exact same crack her father designed in 1982 — her finger following the path his finger made 40 years ago.

**Why it works silently:** Clay, hands, fire, cracks — every element is visual and tactile. The "failed" pots are the Axiom 3 interface: the father encoded a message in the cracks, and the daughter reads it through her fingers. No words — just touch across time.

## #11 — BẢY BÀN THỜ / Seven Altars
`Source: canonical_10_slate_v1.md · 5.0/5 (rescored)`
`1 location · 1 actor · 0–2 lines · Huế`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — A woman moves through 7 rooms of an old Huế garden house. Each room has an altar. Camera placed at each altar's POV — she is always being "watched."
- 1:00–2:00 — She stands frozen before one altar. Her hand reaches toward a job offer letter on the table. Withdraws. Reaches. Withdraws. The altar "watches."
- 2:00–3:00 — She begins to act: carrying the altars one by one into a single room. Physical labor. Sweat. Determination. 7 altars now share one space.
- 3:00–4:00 — She lights 7 incense sticks — one for each. Whispers something to each. The smoke from 7 sticks rises and merges.
- 4:00–5:00 — She picks up her bag. Turns off the light. The door closes. In the dark room, 7 red incense dots glow. They are no longer watching her — they are keeping the house for her.

**Central image:** A dark room with 7 incense dots glowing red, and the sound of a door closing — the ancestors are now guardians, not jailers.

**Why it works near-silently:** The physical action of carrying and rearranging altars IS the psychological transformation. Moving objects = moving internal relationships. No dialogue needed to explain "I feel trapped by 7 generations." We SEE her trapped in 7 POV shots, then we SEE her free herself by bringing them together.

## #12 — HƯƠNG ƯỚC NGÀY MƯA / The Rain Treaty
`Source: script_ideas_v2.md · 5.0/5`
`1 location · 2 actors · 0–3 lines · Miền Trung`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — Storm. An old ancestral hall. Grandfather (75) and granddaughter (24) trapped together. They haven't spoken properly in years. Rain hammers the roof.
- 1:00–2:00 — The roof begins to give way. Water pours in. They scramble to save the ancestral tablets and the old village covenant book (Hương ước).
- 2:00–3:30 — They use their bodies to shield the tablets. Grandfather's old scars visible — from the 1999 flood. Granddaughter sees them for the first time. She touches his scarred arm. He looks at her hands — holding the tablets exactly as his mother held them in 1974.
- 3:30–4:30 — The storm peaks. They are pressed together, holding the tablets between them. Two bodies, three generations of tablets, one covenant book.
- 4:30–5:00 — Dawn. The storm has passed. The roof is gone. But the tablets are dry. They sit in silence among the wreckage. She leans her head on his shoulder.

**Central image:** Two bodies — old and young — pressed together in a storm, holding ancestral tablets between them, keeping 300 years of names dry with nothing but their skin.

**Why it works near-silently:** The storm is the antagonist. The tablets are the stakes. The scars are the backstory. The physical act of shielding tablets together IS the reconciliation — no need to say "I understand you now." The bodies say it.

## #13 — NGHE THẤY NHỊP NẾP CŨ / The Invariant Beat
`Source: script_ideas_v2.md · 4.0/5`
`2 locations · 1 actor · 0 dialogue · Hà Nội Phố Cổ`

**5-min structure:**
- 0:00–1:00 — A real estate broker (man, 35) walks through a busy Hà Nội street. Noise: motorbikes, horns, construction. He enters an old house on Hàng Bạc street. Inside: sudden quiet.
- 1:00–2:00 — He walks through the house — ancestral hall, old silver-engraving tools on the wall. He touches nothing. He's here to convince his aunt to sell.
- 2:00–3:00 — He stops. A sound. Faint at first — a rhythmic hammering. Tink-tink-tink. He looks around. No one there. The sound grows louder. It's coming from inside his head.
- 3:00–4:00 — He stands frozen in the ancestral hall. The hammering rhythm — his grandfather's silver-engraving rhythm — fills the space. His breathing syncs to it. His hand, involuntarily, begins to tap the same rhythm on his thigh.
- 4:00–5:00 — He leaves the house. The sound fades but doesn't disappear. On the street, among the motorbike noise, he still hears it — faint, persistent, the invariant beat. He doesn't go to his aunt's house. He goes back inside.

**Central image:** A man standing frozen in an empty ancestral hall, hearing a hammer that isn't there — his hand tapping a rhythm he never learned, a rhythm his body has always known.

**Why it works silently (4.0 not 5.0):** Sound design does the narrative work — the hammering rhythm IS the Axiom 3 interface. But unlike the Tier 1 scripts, this one relies heavily on audio to carry meaning. Strong visual elements (the empty hall, the tools, the hand tapping) but the sound is the primary storyteller.
